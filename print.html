Linear Algebra

Linear Algebra is the first fundamental pillar of mathematics that supports many engineering domains, primarily because many calculations can be reduced to matrix vector multiplication or matrix multiplications, making them significantly more easier to manipulate.

What makes it truly fundamental is, the tendency to linearize any system around a fixed point, or just linearizing as an approximation to make calculations and analysis more tractible inevitably imports the concepts from linear algebra into the picture.

From a pure math point of view, the fundamental object of study in Linear Algebra is: Vector Space. Everything in linear algebra revolves around vector spaces including the fact that vector spaces with one interesting property, automatically becomes amenable to computation and constructive proofs inevitably become algorithms. Hopefully, for people thinking, "another theory with no use", this sparks some motivation.

1. Vector Spaces

Before delving into what a vector space is, it is necessary to see what are the components that make a vector space.

1.1. Fields

Definition 1.1 [Fields]

A collection of objects F endowed with operations +,⋅ (addition and multiplication respectively) denoted as F≡(F,+,⋅) is a field if it satisfies the following conditions:

  1. [Closure]: ∀a,b∈F

    1. a+b∈F
    2. a⋅b∈F

  2. [Commutativity]: ∀a,b∈F

    1. a+b=b+a
    2. a⋅b=b⋅a

  3. [Associativity]: ∀a,b,c∈F

    1. a+(b+c)=(a+b)+c
    2. a⋅(b⋅c)=(a⋅b)⋅c

  4. [Identities]: ∃0,1∈F such that:

    1. ∀a∈F,a+0=a
    2. ∀a∈F,a⋅1=a

  5. [Inverses]:

    1. ∀a∈F,∃−a∈F:a+(−a)=0
    2. ∀a∈F−{0},∃a−1:a⋅a−1=1

  6. [Distributivity]: ∀a,b,c∈F,

    • a⋅(b+c)=a⋅b+a⋅c

Each property is important and the loss of even one of them will mean NOT a field.

Common examples of fields are:

  1. Rational Numbers Q under usual +,⋅
  2. Real Numbers R under usual +,⋅
  3. Complex Numbers C under usual +,⋅
  4. Boolean Field F2​≡({0,1},⊕,⋅) where (⊕,⋅) are XOR & AND operations respectively.

The point with all these properties is, you don't need to remember them. Almost all of them are something which is true in rationals, reals and complex numbers. But the point is, they apply to all fields.

Like, multiplication with 0 and -1, commonly used everywhere, is a general property of fields, not just a special case. The not so obvious and theoretical ones are last two, which are inequality of the identity elements. Because of this one property, any field must at least have two elements 0 & 1. That smallest field is F2​ which has only 0,1 as elements and satisfies field definition under XOR and AND as the two operations.

1.2 Vector Spaces

Definition 1.2 [Vector Space]

A collection of objects V endowed with operations +,⋅ (vector addition and scalar multiplication respectively), over a field F≡(F,+^​,⋅^) denoted as V(F)≡(V,+,⋅,F) is a vector space if it satisfies the following conditions:

  1. [Closure]: ∀u,v∈V, c∈F

    1. u+v∈V
    2. c⋅v∈V

  2. [Commutativity]: ∀u,v∈V, a∈F

    1. u+v=v+u
    2. c⋅v=v⋅c

  3. [Associativity]: ∀u,v,w∈V & a,b∈F

    1. u+(v+w)=(v+u)+w
    2. a⋅(b⋅v)=(a⋅^b)⋅v

  4. [Identities]

    1. ∃0V​:∀v∈V,v+0V​=v
    2. ∀v∈V,1F​⋅v=v,  where 1F​ is the mult. identity from F

  5. [Inverses]

    • ∀v∈V,∃−v∈V :v+(−v)=0V​

  6. [Distributivity]: ∀u,v∈V & a,b∈F

    1. a⋅(u+v)=a⋅u+ a⋅v
    2. (a+^​b)⋅v=a⋅v+b⋅v

Six conditions, just like fields, with some minor changes. This definition fits any abstract thing you might choose to call a vector.

The second thing is, the definition of commutativity differs from the standard textbook definition. I define it this way because I respect symmetry 😉.

Common examples of fields are:

  1. Fn under elementwise addition ⊕ & elementwise scalar multiplication ⊙ is a vector space over F≡(F,+^​,⋅^). Usually F=R,C,Q,{0,1} are encountred in Physics, Coding Theory, Deep Learning or any other domain using vectors.

  2. Pm​={∑i=0m​ai​⋅zi: ai​∈F∀i=0,1,...,m}, the set of all polynomials with degree less than or equal to m, and coefficients from F, with usual addition and scalar multiplication (+,⋅) is a vector space over F≡(F,+^​,⋅^).

  3. FX≡F(X,F), the set of all possible function from a non-empty set X to a set F under pointwise addition + & pointwise multiplication ⋅ is a vector space over F≡(F,+^​,⋅^).

Among these, the first one is the most important because it is the most amenable to computation.

1.3 Subspaces

Definition 1.3 [Subspace]

  1. Given V(F) is a vector space, any U ⊂V is a subspace of V, if U is a vector space over F under the same vector addition and scalar multiplication as V(F).

    It is denoted as U(F)≤V(F) ≡U≤V.

  2. U is a proper subspace of V if U⊊V and U(F) ≤V(F), i.e. U is strictly contained within V and is a vector space in itself.

    It is denoted as U(F)<V(F) ≡U<V.

The reason for subspace will become pretty clear after a property. The concept of subspace reduces alot of effort in proving something is a bonafide vector space.

Physically, the concept of subspace reduces the no.of components that is required to represent a vector. Principle Component Analysis (PCA) is this exact line of thought put into action.

The whole concept of Eigenvalues rest upon your understanding of subspaces. So, better know how to use this.

1.4 Subset Sum

P-1.3.3 leaves you hanging on a question, given two subspaces, if intersection can be used to find smaller subspaces [P-1.3.2], then how do we create bigger subspaces?

The answer to that is: Subset Sum!

Definition 1.4 [Subset Sum]

Given subspaces of V(F), U1​,U2​,...,Un​≤V:

  1. U1​+U2​+...+Un​≡∑i=1n​Ui​ ≜{u1​+u2​+...+un​: ui​∈Ui​∀i=1,2,...n}

  2. If every v∈∑i=1n​Ui​, ∃!ui​,i=1,2,...,n:v=∑i=1n​ui​, then ∑i=1n​Ui​ is a direct sum and is then denoted by ⨁i=1n​Ui​.

This looks like one of those esoteric things, that just makes a cameo in some analyses and just doesn't come back again, but this is much more than that.

The use case comes about when instead of looking at adding up some given subspaces, you start looking at how a given subset can be broken down into a subset sum (much better, a direct sum).

For example [looking into the future for motivation here]:

  1. Eigenspaces & Matrix Diagonalization hinge upon decomposition of columns space of matrix into a direct sum.

  2. Quantum Mechanics frequently uses decomposition of particle states into direct sums to separate out useful information from them.

  3. Fourier Analysis & Signal Processing is literally decomposing a signal space into sum of subspaces where signals of a single frequency live. Most basic use case of subset sum.

1.5 Lattices

In the last sections, we discovered the intersection operation which allows us to create the largest contained subspace in a list of subspaces P-1.3.4 + Induction, and the subset sum operation which creates the smallest containing subspace.

Notice that these form a greatest lower bound (glb) and least upper bound (lub) operations on subspaces, respectively. But lub and glb require some sort of ordering, which is what is provided by subspace inclusion (≤).

Hence, the collection of all the subspaces of a vector space V(F), call it S(V) ≡{U:U≤V} forms a special structure with the subspace inclusion, called a Lattice.

There are two definitions of lattice, one algebraic and one from Order Theory. We'll show that under both of them, S(V) is a lattice. Even otherwise, both the definitions of lattice are equivalent. This is just a small analysis, usually not taught in a linear algebra course.

Definition 1.5 [Lattice]

  1. [Parital Ordering] Given a set S, a binary relation ≤ is called a partial ordering on S if it satisfies the following conditions:

    1. [Reflexivity] ∀x∈S,x≤x
    2. [Antisymmetry] ∀x,y∈S, x≤y,y≤x⟹x=y
    3. [Transistivity] ∀x,y,z∈S, x≤y,y≤z⟹x≤z

    And (S,≤) is called a Poset [Set with partial ordering].

  2. [Lattice: Order Theory] A poset (S,≤) is a lattice if there exist two operations, defined for all possible a,b∈S:

    1. ∧ (glb, infimum or meet): The Greatest Lower Bound of a,b∈S,a∧b is the largest element in S that is smaller than a,b.

    2. ∨ (lub, supremum or join): The Least Upper Bound of a,b∈S,a∨b is the smallest element in S that is larger than both a,b.

  3. [Lattice: Algebraic] A collection of objects S endowed with two operations meet(∧) and join(∨) forms a lattice (S,∧,∨) if it satisfies the following:

    1. [Closure]: ∀a,b∈S,
      1. a∧b∈S
      2. a∨b∈S
    2. [Commutativity] ∀a,b∈S,
      1. a∧b=b∧a
      2. a∨b=b∨a
    3. [Associativity] ∀a,b,c∈S,
      1. a∧(b∧c)=(a∧b)∧c
      2. a∨(b∨c)=(a∨b)∨c
    4. [Idempotence] ∀a∈S
      1. a∧a=a
      2. a∨a=a
    5. [Absorption] ∀a,b∈S
      1. a∧(a∨b)=a
      2. a∨(a∧b)=a
