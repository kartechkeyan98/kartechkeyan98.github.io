2.1. Span

Definition 2.1 [Linear Combination; Span]

1. [Linear Combination]
Given a finite list of vectors L=(v1​,v2​,⋯vn​), and scalars A=(a1​,a2​,⋯an​)∈Fn, linear combination of L with coefficients in A is:

u=a1​⋅v1​+a2​⋅v2​+⋯+an​⋅vn​≡i=1∑n​ai​⋅vi​​

2. [Finite Spanning Set/Span]
Given a finite list of vectors from V, L=(v1​,v2​,⋯vn​), the finite spanning set or span(L) is the set of all possible linear combinations of L:

span(L)={i=1∑n​ai​⋅vi​:ai​∈F∀i=1,2,⋯n}​

  • If span(L)=V, we say that L spans V.
  • vectors in L are called generators of span(L).

Given V(F)≡ (V,+,⋅,F) is a vector space over the field F≡(F,+^​,⋅^).

These properties are not random. They have an order in which they can appear.

1. [Synthesis]
In the subset sum definition, the simple question was that if given multiple subspaces, is a bigger vector space synthesizable such that it contains the the smaller ones.
The question here is simpler, given a list of vectors, what is the way to create a subspace containing all of them. The answer is span and the bonus is that it is the smallest containing vector space. This is covered in:

  • P-2.1.1 [Smallest Containing Subspace]

2. [Alteration]
View span as an operator. A function that takes in a list of vectors of arbitrary size and produces the smallest vector space containing them all. How will altering the list by one element alter the span, is the question.

  • P-2.1.2 [Adding/Removing Dependent Vectors]
  • P-2.1.2 [Adding/Removing Independent Vectors]

These two properties basically tell when the span is preserved, leading to the concept of dependent and independent vectors.
Span is preserved iff dependent vectors are added or removed while doing the same with indepedent vectors for sure alter the span, what relation the altered span will have comes about from P-2.1.4, which states that span is monotonic.

3. [Combination]
This is one level above alteration. Instead of altering list one vector at a time, the two ways in which two lists can be combined or have a relation is:

  1. Subset: P-2.1.4 [Monotonicity of Span]
  2. Union: P-2.1.5.1 [Span of Union]
  3. Intersection: P-2.1.5.2 [Span of Intersection]

These properties deal with that.

2.2 Linear Independence [LI]

Definition 2.1 [Linear Independence (LI)]

Given a list of vectors L=(v1​,⋯vn​) from V, L is Linearly Independent (LI) if:

∃!{ai​∈F}i=1n​:0V​=i=1∑n​ai​⋅vi​

Meaning, 0V​ is uniquely expressible as a lienar combination of the vectors in the list L.

Some common sense out of this:

  • If L is not LI, it is Linearly Dependent (LD)
  • The empty list ϕ=() is LI by default and span(ϕ)={0V​}.
  • In LI list, the unique linear combination is all zeros.
  • In LD list, a non-zero combination exists which add up to 0V​.
  • The all zeros combination is called trivial linear combination.

Properties of Linear Independence [P-2.2]

Given V(F)≡ (V,+,⋅,F) is a vector space over the field F≡(F,+^​,⋅^).

1. [Uniqueness of Coefficients]

Let L be a finite list of vectors from V,
L is LI ⟺ every vector in span(L) has unique coefficients of linear combination.

NOTE: This uniqueness implies a bijection between Fn & span(L).

Proof

(⟹) Let L be LI. Then, 0V​ is uniquely expressible as only the trivial linear combination, by Def. 2.2.

Take any x∈span(L). Suppose it can be expressed in two ways:

∴∴∴∴∴∴∴​x=i=1∑n​ai​⋅vi​⟶Def. 2.1.2, span=i=1∑n​bi​⋅vi​x+(−x)=i=1∑n​ai​⋅vi​+−i=1∑n​bi​⋅vi​0V​=i=1∑n​ai​⋅vi​+−1F​⋅i=1∑n​bi​⋅vi​→Def. 1.2.5, add. inv.P-1.2.5, mult. with −1F​0V​=i=1∑n​ai​⋅vi​+−1F​⋅(bi​⋅vi​)→Def. 1.2.6.1, Distribitivity &Def. 1.2.2.1, commutativity of + repeated0V​=i=1∑n​(ai​+^​(−1F​⋅^bi​))⋅vi​→Def. 1.2.3.2, Associativity of ⋅& Def. 1.2.6.2, distributivity0V​=i=1∑n​(ai​+^​(−bi​))⋅vi​→P-1.1.7, mult. with −1ai​+^​(−bi​)=0F​∀i=1,2,⋯n⟶Def. 2.2, LIai​=−(−bi​)=bi​∀i=1,2,⋯n⟶Def. 1.1.5.1, add inv.P-1.1.3.1, inverse of inverse ​

This means that any set of coefficients found to express x as linear combination of L is the set of coefficients and is unique.

(⟸) If every vector in span(L) has unique coefficients, since span is a subspace, by P-2.1.1, it should contain 0V​, Def. 1.2.4.1, additive identity.
Then, by what is given, 0V​ has is uniquely expressible, meaning L is LI from Def. 2.2.

Hence, generator is LI iff coefficients in span are unique. q.e.d

2. [Array Representation of Vectors]

Given any finite list L which is LI, has size n.

1. Any vector in span(L) can be represented by a unique array of numbers in Fn.
2. Operations + and ⋅ in span(L) are equivalent to :

  • ⊕:Fn×Fn ↦Fn
    (c1​,c2​,⋯cn​)⊕(d1​,d2​,⋯dn​)
    =(c1​+^​d1​,⋯cn​+^​dn​)

  • ⊙:F×Fn ↦Fn and ⊙:Fn ×F↦Fn
    c⊙(x1​,x2​,⋯xn​)= (x1​,x2​,⋯xn​)⊙c
    =(c⋅^x1​,⋯c⋅^xn​)

3. Fn(F) ≡(Fn,⊕,⊙,F) is a bonafide vector space.
4. span(L)≅Fn, i.e both are isomorphic to each other.

Proof

1. By P-2.2.1, since coefficients of span(L) is unique, and there are n coefficients in total. Hence, each vector in span(L) can be represented by a unique n-tuple from Fn.

2. Given x,y∈span(L), by P-2.2.2.1, we can find xF​,yF​ ∈Fn, which are their equivalent representations s.t.

x=∑i=1n​xF,i​⋅vi​y=∑i=1n​yF,i​⋅vi​​}P-2.2.2.1

x+y can be written as:

∴​x+y=i=1∑n​xF,i​⋅vi​+i=1∑n​yF,i​⋅vi​x+y=i=1∑n​(xF,i​+^​yF,i​)⋅vi​→Def. 1.2.2.1, commutativity of +& Def. 1.2.6.2, Distributivity​

Hence, the equivalent representation of x+y is : xF​⊕yF​ from the above.

c⋅x for any c∈F can be written as :

c⋅x​=c⋅i=1∑n​xF,i​⋅vi​=i=1∑n​(c⋅^xF,i​)⋅vi​→Def. 1.2.6.1, Distributivity &Def. 1.2.3.2, Associativity of ⋅​

Hence, the equivalent representation of c⋅x is : c⊙xF​ by the above.
∴⊕,⊙ are the equivalent of vector add. & scalar mult. in Fn space.

3. This part was actually mentioned in one of the examples below Definition-1.2, that Fn(F) is a bonafide vector space under the elmentwise add. and mult. operations.

4. To establish an isomorphism between span(L) and Fn, two conditions must be satisfied:

  1. Bijectivity: A one-to-one and onto mapping must exist between the two spaces.
  2. Linearity: The mapping must preserve vector addition and scalar multiplication, which was established in P-2.2.2.2.

By P-2.2.1, because L is linearly independent, every vector x∈span(L) can be uniquely represented by a coordinate vector xF​∈Fn. This uniqueness guarantees that the coordinate mapping ϕ:span(L)→Fn is injective (span(L)↪Fn).

Conversely, every coefficient vector xF​∈Fn maps to a vector in span(L) via the linear combination:

x=i=1∑n​xF,i​vi​

Because L is linearly independent, distinct coefficient vectors cannot map to the same vector in span(L). This establishes an injection in the opposite direction (Fn↪span(L)). Since the mapping is both well-defined for all inputs and injective, it constitutes a bijection.

Hence, isomorphism between the two vector spaces is established, span(L)≅Fn

q.e.d

7. [Steinitz Exchange Lemma]

Given L=(v1​,⋯vn​) which spans V and S=(w1​,⋯wm​) which is LI. Then:

1. m≤n [any LI list cannot be longer than a spanning list]
2. ∃L′⊂L:∣L′∣=n−m and span(S∪L′)=V
[Can exchange vectors in L with those in S and preserve span].

Proof
The proof for this comes about algorithmically. Two operations are required for this :

  1. Span Membership in_span(x,L) checks if x is in the span of L by returning nontrivial coefficients (a1​,⋯an​)∈Fn if it does belong to the span else returns all-zeros.

  2. Dependence Removal find_dependent(L) will give the index j s.t. vj​∈span(v1:j−1​) if such a j exists, else returns null. This is algorithm 2.1.

Algorithm 2.2 [Steinitz Exchange Algorithm]

Input

  • L=(v1​,⋯vn​) is a list which spans V
  • S=(w1​,⋯wm​) is an LI list of vectors from V

Output

  • T is a list which spans V and contains all elements of S exchanged with elements of L and spans V

Algorithm

  1. T(0)←L
  2. for i=1 to m do
    1. R(i)=insert(wi​,T(i−1),i)
      This inserts wi​ as the i-th element in the list
    2. j←find_dependent(R(i))
    3. T(i)←R(i)−{rj(i)​}
      rj(i)​ is the j-th element in R(i)
  3. end for
  4. return T(m)

The algorithm's correctness will be proof of the theorem. Three things are required for the correctness:

  1. T(m) should span V
  2. T(m) should contain all the elements in S
  3. T(m) should have same length as L

Initialization : T(0)=L spans V is given.
Iterations: Suppose that T(i−1) spans V, then

​wi​=j=1∑n​aj(i−1)​⋅tj(i−1)​→tj(i−1)​≜j-th element in T(i−1)⟶Def. 2.1.2, span​

This combination will never be trivial since wi​ ∈V=span(T(i−1)) and since wi​ is from an LI list S, it cannot be zero as well, by P-2.2.3. Hence, no possibility of a trivial combination at all.

Step 2.1
After inserting wi​ as first element in the list, we have
R(i)=(t1(i−1)​,⋯ti−1(i−1)​,wi​⋯tn(i−1)​). This makes R(i) an LD list since adding a new vector from span of T(i−1) makes it LD, by P-2.2.5.2.

Step 2.2
This is the tricky part, because the way T(i) evolves is :

  1. R(1)={w1​,⋯}. find_dependent acting on this list will never remove w1​ since it does not belong to span(r1:0(i−1)​)= span(ϕ)={0V​}. Hence, T(1) will most definitely contain w1​.
  2. R(2)=(w1​,w2​,⋯). Again w1​ or w2​ cannot be removed since they come from an LI list S and by contrapositive of LD Lemma [P-2.2.6], it neither can belong to the span of the previous. Hence, T(2) contains both.

Doing this for i iterations, R(i)=(w1​⋯wi​⋯) and none of the first i elements can be removed due to contrapositive of LD Lemma, P-2.2.6. Hence, T(i) contains w1​ to wi​.

Step 2.3
By LD Lemma P-2.2.6.2, find_dependent will find a vector in R(i) which originally belonged to L and will only remove that, hence, exchanging a vector in L with one from S. The removal of that vector rj(i)​ will not affect the span and span of T(i) hence will still be V and will also have same length as L since step 2.2 inserts a vector and step 2.3 removes another. Hence,

  1. span(T(i))=V
  2. ∣T(i)∣=n

This hence, proves the correctness of algorithm 2.2, since T(m) will span V, contains all the elements of S and has length n.

Hence, through the correctness of algorithm 2.2, it is established that an alternative list L′ can be constructed such that is contains all elements of S and n−m elements of L with span (S∪L′)=V.

This proves P-2.2.7.2 provided that m≤n. That will be the case always because if m>n, then at some point, T(n) will become full of vectors from S and R(n+1)=T(n)∪{wn+1​} ⊂S should be LD by the above proof.
But that would mean P-2.2.4.2 [Subset of LI is LI] would not hold, hence, contradicting LI nature of S.

Hence, m≤n always, and exchange is possible via algorithm 2.2. q.e.d

There is a story to this as well, a much more deeper one that leads to something good.

1. [Uniqueness]
Span contains every possible linear combination. But a question arises, is the linear combination unique? When is it unique?
Answer: When Generator List is LI. This leads to properties:

  • P-2.2.1 : LI ⟺ Uniqueness of Coefficients
  • P-2.2.2 : Span of any LI list is isomorphic to Fn (F)

P-2.2.2 can also e stated as: the coefficients in the span of LI generators form a vector space themselves over the same field. This is the biggest takeaway from here, as it is what justifies representing vectors with a list of numbers.

Examples are:

  1. Vectors in physics denoted as a linear combination of i^, j^​, k^, the unit vectors in direction of x,y,z axes respectively. These vectors, they depend on how the the directions are chosen. Any three arbitrary directions can be chosen as x,y,z axes, but two sets of those axes are not the same. Their i^, j^​, k^ represent two very different directions in reality.

    But all these vectors may be represented using a tuple from R3, in which case, both those vector spaces with abstract unit direction vectors become isomorphic to R3.

  2. Solutions to the linear ODE y′′+y=0 form a 2-dimensional vector space (closed under addition and scalar multiplication). Two natural bases for this space are

    {cosx,sinx}and{eix,e−ix}.

    Under the first basis, the tuple (1,0) represents the function cosx; under the second, the same tuple (1,0) represents the function eix instead. There is no ambient coordinate grid here to fall back on, since the vectors themselves are functions — the tuple exists only because a basis was chosen, and a different choice of basis gives a different tuple for the same vector.

  3. The space P2​(F) of polynomials of degree at most 2 is 3-dimensional. The standard basis {1,x,x2} represents a polynomial as the tuple of its coefficients. But given three points x0​,x1​,x2​, the Lagrange basis {L0​,L1​,L2​}, where Li​ is the unique degree-2 polynomial with Li​(xj​)=δij​, represents the same polynomial as the tuple of its values at x0​,x1​,x2​. The two tuples for a given polynomial look nothing alike, yet both arise from the same isomorphism to F3, applied with two different bases.

The punchline is : the tuples which are colloquially called vectors exist merely as representation of abstract vectors. The LI generators underlying them are usually hidden and the only thing that matters is what happens as the tuples are operated on.

This is where computational linear algebra starts. It always assumes the existence of some abstract vector space and all the vectors are represented and processed as tuple/array of numbers, without questioning their consequences in the abstract vector space. Only point of matter there is what happens in Fn.

2. [Alteration]
The question asked now is how does altering a generator list affect LI/LD?
This is covered by:

  • P-2.2.3, zero vector in a generator always makes it LD.
  • P-2.2.4, subset of LI is LI & superset of LD is LD.
  • P-2.2.5, adding independent vectors preserves LI & adding dependent vectors breaks it.

3. [Optimization]
For lists that span V, is it really necessary to have a huge list? Is it possible to reduce it somehow? This is covered by:

  • P-2.2.6, the LD Lemma along with Algorithm 2.1.
  • P-2.2.7, Steinitz Exchange Lemma and Algorithm 2.2.

NOTE

Applying LD Lemma, P-2.2.6 repeatedly to an LD list that spans V allows you to keep on reducing the generator list whilst preserving the spanning property of the list.
But it is Steinitz Lemma that puts a lower bound on how small the size of the list can get before it stops spanning V.

This leads to the concept of Minimal Spanning Set, i.e. the smallest set that spans V.

There is a second optimization problem that is equally interesting. Given an LI list, how much can it be extended before it becomes LD? The same two properties cover this as well.

NOTE

Applying P-2.2.5.1, adding independent vectors will expand the span and preserve LI. So, LI can be preserved whilst expanding the generator list.
But again it is Steinitz Lemma, P-2.2.7 that puts an upper bound on how big the size of the LI list can get.

This leads to the concept of Maximal LI Set in V.

The interesting thing is: the solution to both problems are a special class of generator lists known as : Basis, which is the topic of the next section.

2.3 Basis

Definition 2.3 [Finite Dim. Vector Spaces; Basis]

1. [FDVS] A vector space V(F) is Finite Dimensional if there exisits a finite generator list such that it span V.

2. [Basis] A basis of a vector space V(F) is a list of vectors which span V and are LI.

The existence of LI sets is not an issue. But the existence of spanning set may not always be guaranteed, which is why the concept of Finite Dimensional Vector Spaces [FDVS] comes in. It guarantees a finite generator list, which is also a spanning set!!

This also guarantees the existence of the solution to both problems: Maximal LI set & Minimal Spanning Set. The solution to both is the special class of generators that are called the basis of V.

Properties of Basis [P-2.3]

Given V(F)≡ (V,+,⋅,F) is a vector space over the field F≡(F,+^​,⋅^) and V(F) is finite dimensional.

1. [Criterion for Basis]

A list L=(v1​,⋯vn​) is a basis for an FDVS V(F) iff ∀u∈V, ∃!{ai​∈F}i​ :u=∑i=1n​ai​⋅vi​.

Proof

(⟹) If L is a basis, it is LI and spans V, by definition 2.3.2, basis. Hence, every vector in span(L)=V will have unique set of coefficients, by P-2.2.1.

(⟸) If every vector in V can be expressed as a unique linear combination of L, then by P-2.2.1, L is LI and hence, V⊂span(L).
But, since L contains vectors from V, and by P-2.1.1, span(L) is a subspace of V, hence V⊃span(L).
Hence, V=span(L) and L is LI, which by definition 2.3.2 makes it a basis.

2. [Minimal Spanning & Maximal LI sets]

1. If L is a minimal spanning list, it also LI
2. If L is a maximal LI list, it is also spanning.

Proof

NOTE

  1. LD Lemma + Steinitz + FD showed that finite spanning lists exist & there is a minimality to them, governed by LI list sizes.

  2. P-2.2.5.1 + Steinitz + FD show that LI lists also exist and there is a maximality to their size goverened by spanning list sizes.

This property basically shows, the solution to both problems is the same thing: BASIS.

1. Say L is a minimal spanning list.
If L was not LI, then by LD Lemma [P-2.2.6], some vk​∈L can be kicked out whilst preserving the span, in which case a smaller spanning list has been found, contradicting the minimality of L.
Hence, L is also LI. q.e.d.

2. Suppose L is a maximal LI set.
Say it does not span V, i.e. span(L)⊊V, then some v∈V−span(L) can be found. By P-2.2.5.1, addition of independent vectors results in another larger LI list, which contradicts the maximality of L.
Hence, L must span V.q.e.d

3. [Basis is Minimal Spanning & Maximal LI list]

Any Basis B of an FDVS V(F) is both Minimal Spanning & Maximal LI list.

Proof

B is a basis, hence both spanning and LI, by definition 2.3.2.

  • Since B is LI, by P-2.1.3.2, removal of any vector will result in span shrinking, since all of them are independent. This means that B is a minimal spanning set as no vector can be removed without shirnking span.

  • Since B is a spanning set, the only set from which independent vectors can be chosen from is : V− span(B)=ϕ, i.e. no more independent vectors exist to add to B and preserve LI, which by definiton also makes B a maximal LI set.

Hence, any basis B is both a maximal LI and minimal spanning set. q.e.d.

Minimal Spanning ⟺ Basis ⟺ Maximal LI

4. [Existence of Basis]

For any finte dimensional vector space V(F), ∃ a finite list B, which is a basis.

Proof

There are two ways of proving this, both of which are constructive.

1. [Top Down Approach]
By virtue of finite dimensionality of V, there exists a finite spanning list L=(v1​,⋯vn​), by definition 2.3.1.

Algorithm 2.3 [Discard/Reduction Algorithm] reduce(L,V)

Input

  • L≡(v1​,⋯vn​), a list spanning V

Output

  • S=(x1​,⋯xm​), a basis with elements from L.

Algorithm

  1. i←0

  2. L(0)←L

  3. while true do

    1. j=find_dependent(L)
    2. if j=null then
      This is entered iff all dependent vectors are removed.
      1. break
    3. end if
    4. L(i+1)=L(i)−{lj(i)​}
      Discard dependent vector in the list
    5. i←i+1

  4. end while

  5. return L(i)

Proof of Correctness:

  • This algorithm starts with a finite spanning set of V guaranteed to exist due to Finite Dimensionality [Def. 2.3.1].
  • Inside the while loop, if L(i−1) is LD, j will not be null, via algorithm 2.1, which proceeds to remove a dependent vector in L(i−1), whilst preserving the span, P-2.2.6, LD Lemma.
  • The while loop will definitely terminate since, the minimum size of an LI list is 1 [choose any non-zero vector and it is an LI list]. P-2.2.7, Steinitz Exchange Lemma guarantees that a spanning list cannot be smaller than an LI list, hence, a definite lower bound is present.
  • Overall, the algorithm produces a minimal spanning set, which by P-2.2.3 is a Basis!

q.e.d

2. [Bottom Up Approach]
Suppose an already LI list is found L, which is always guaranteed to exist in any vector space, the following algorithm produces a basis out of that.

Algorithm 2.4 [Basis Extension Algorithm] extend(L,V)

Input

  • L an already LI list

Output

  • S, a basis formed by extending L

Algorithm

  1. L(0)←L
  2. i←0
  3. while V−span(L(i))=ϕ do
    1. find vi​∈V−span(L(i))
    2. L(i+1)←L(i)∪{vi​}
    3. i←i+1
  4. end while
  5. return L(i)

Correctness Proof:

  • Each iteration, an independent vector is being added to L(i), meaning the resultant list will still be LI, by P-2.2.5.1.
  • This while loop will eventually terminate since P-2.2.7, Steinitz Lemma guarantees an upper bound on the size of an LI list, which is the size of any spanning list, which exists due to V being FD.
  • The final output after the loop terminates, is a maximal LI list, which by P-2.3.3 is a Basis!

q.e.d

Corollary 4.1
Any spanning list L can be reduced to a basis

Corollary 4.2
Any LI list can be extended to an basis

Algorithm 2.3 & 2.4 essentially give you the algorithm to solve the optimization problems of maximal LI list and minimal spanning list.

5. [Subspace of an FDVS]

Any subspace of a finite dimensional vector space is also finite dimensional.

Proof

Given U≤V is a subspace of an already FD vector space. The proof here is also contructive

Using algorithm 2.4, use extend(ϕ,U). If U={0V​}. At every step, L(i) will be an LI list from U≤V.

Hence, since V is a finite dimensional list, it will have a finite spanning set of size n, which is the upper bound on number of iterations in extend(ϕ,U).

Hence, it ends with a finite LI list that spans U, hence by definition 2.3.1, making it FD.

q.e.d

6. [Complementary Subspace]

Given any U≤V, there always exists a W≤V such that U⊕W=V.
W is called the complementary subspace of U.

Proof

Since V is FD, by P-2.2.5, U is also FD.

  • Hence, by P-2.2.4, a basis for U, call it BU​ exists.

  • By algorithm 2.4, since BU​ is LI (It is a basis, will be LI by definition 2.3.2), extend(BU​,V) gives an extended basis which spans V, call it:

    BV​=BU​∪BW​

  • Since, BV​ is LI, BW​⊂BV​ is also LI and forms the basis for another subspace, call it W, by P-2.2.4.1.

Any vector in V is hence, written as:

v=∈Ui∑​ai​⋅bu,i​​​+∈Wi∑​bi​⋅bw,i​​​⟶Def. 2.1.2, span​

By definition 1.4, subset sum, V=U+W, but to prove that this is a direct sum, notice that since BV​ is a basis, no vector in BW​ is in the span of BU​ which is U, by contrpositive of LD Lemma P-2.2.6.

Hence, the only common element in U,W is 0V​, hence by P-1.4.3, U∩W={0V​}⟹ U+W=U⊕W=Vq.e.d

7. [All basis have same length]

Any basis of V will have the same length

Proof

Suppose B1​,B2​ are two basis of V with length m,n.

  • By definition 2.3.2, B1​ is spanning and B2​ is an LI list, hence by P-2.2.7, Steinitz Lemma, n≤m
  • By definition 2.3.2, B2​ is spanning and B1​ is an LI list, hence by P-2.2.7, Steinitz Lemma, m≤n

Combining both of them gives m=n,q.e.d

The story around this starts from proving the equivalence of the solutions to the two optimization problems.

1. [Equivalence]

Both maximal LI set and minimal spanning set are equivalent and they both are a basis.
Hence, these three are different faces of the same object, just that they come about in different ways.

This is covered by properties:

  1. P-2.3.1, characterization of basis in terms of LI and span
  2. P-2.3.2 which states the equivalence between maximal LI and minimal spanning set.
  3. P-2.3.3 which states the equivalence between basis and the other two.

3. [Existence]
The next question is, when does the basis even exist? The answer is, for a finite dimensional vector space, always. The whole concept of finite dimensional was brought in for the sole purpose of guranteeing the existence of a finite spanning set. This is covered in :

  • P-2.3.4, which proves existence with algorithm 2.3, 2.4, which also allows reduction of LD sets and extension of LI sets to generate a basis in any FDVS [Corollary 2.3.4.1, 2.3.4.2].

4. [Subspace]
The effect of FD on subspaces is that they also become FD, and gives rise to the concept of a complementary subspace, which is a subspace that completes V through a direct sum. This gets covered in :

  • P-2.3.5, subspaces which says that subspaces of FDVS is also FD
  • P-2.3.6, complementary subspace which gives a subspace that direct sums to the original larger subspace.

5. [Invariance]
Another important question is, are all minimal spanning, maximal LI sets of the same length? The answer is: YES, which is covered in:

  • P-2.3.7, size invariance property of basis.

The last part of Invariance leads to the concept of a fundmental property of any vector space: Dimension. Infact, during bijective vector space transformations, this number acts as an invariant.

2.4 Dimension

Definition 2.4 [Dimension]

The length of any basis of a vector space V(F), denoted as dim(V) is the dimension of that vector space.

This is the punchline of all the theory developed till now.

Properties of Dimension [P-2.4]

Given V(F)≡ (V,+,⋅,F) is a vector space over the field F≡(F,+^​,⋅^) and V(F) is finite dimensional.
